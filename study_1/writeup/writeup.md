# Loneliness and Willingness to Pay for Kid-Adulting Products
## A Simulated Experimental Study (LLM-Generated Data, N=20)

> **Note**: This study uses LLM-simulated participant responses generated by Gemini-2.5-pro
> as a theory-testing tool, not as a replacement for human participants.

---

## Abstract

Across a simulated experiment with N = 20 participants (loneliness: n = 10;
control: n = 10), we tested whether temporary loneliness increases consumers'
willingness to pay (WTP) for kid-adulting products — items that blend childhood nostalgia or
playful aesthetics with adult functionality. Participants were randomly assigned to write about
either a time they felt lonely or their typical morning routine (control), then evaluated five
kid-adulting products across categories (snacks, apparel, home decor, accessories, beverages).
As predicted, participants in the loneliness condition reported higher WTP (M = $15.45, SD = $2.47) compared to those in the control condition (M = $15.30, SD = $2.44), t = 0.137, p = 0.893, d = 0.061(ns). These effects persisted when controlling for participant demographics and product
identity in an integrated OLS regression with cluster-robust standard errors.
Three mediators (nostalgia, comfort, social-surrogate function) were examined via path analysis.

**Keywords**: loneliness, willingness to pay, kid-adulting, consumer behavior, nostalgia,
comfort seeking, simulated participants, LLM experiment

---

## Introduction

Recent work in consumer psychology suggests that social exclusion and loneliness systematically
shift product preferences toward items that signal connection, comfort, or nostalgia
(Epley et al. 2008; Wan et al. 2014). Kid-adulting products — defined as adult-functional
goods with distinctly childlike or nostalgic aesthetics — represent a naturalistic category
in which these effects may be pronounced: they promise not just utility but an emotional
transport to a less isolated social identity. This study tests whether a temporary loneliness
induction increases WTP for such products, and explores three potential mediation pathways:
nostalgia evoked by the product, comfort-seeking motivation, and social-surrogate function.

## Method

### Participants and Design

N = 20 simulated participants (loneliness: n = 10, control: n = 10)
were generated with randomized demographic profiles (age, gender, annual income, education,
employment, and relationship status) using a fixed random seed (42) to ensure reproducibility.
The study employed a 2 (condition: loneliness vs. control) × 5 (products) mixed
design with condition as the between-subjects factor and products as the within-subjects factor
(100 observations total).

**Sample characteristics** (simulated):
- Age: M = 34.5, SD = 9.8, range 18-48
- Gender: male: 12, female: 6, non-binary: 2
- Annual income: M = $65,966, Median = $63,246

### Stimulus Materials

**Loneliness manipulation**: Participants were asked to write about a specific time they felt
deeply lonely and isolated.

**Control manipulation**: Participants described their typical weekday morning routine.

**Products** (5 kid-adulting products evaluated by all participants):

We've transformed your favorite sugary, star-shaped cereal into a sophisticated snack bar with a creamy vanilla coating and a hint of sea salt. It's a comfortable, high-quality garment that gives a nod to your gaming past without sacrificing modern style.
  3. **Dino-Plant Holder** (Home Decor, $24.99): Bring a touch of prehistoric fun to your workspace or living room with the Dino-Plant Holder. This ceramic planter is shaped like a friendly Brontosaurus, with a hollowed-out back perfectly sized for a small succulent or air plant.   4. **Jelly-Pop Keychain Wallet** (Accessories, $18.0): Remember those glittery jelly sandals? We've captured that '90s magic in the Jelly-Pop Keychain Wallet. ### Measures

**Manipulation checks** (3 items, 1-7 scale): felt lonely, disconnected, isolated (α computed
as composite mean; reported as single latent factor via average).

**Primary DV**: WTP — maximum dollar amount willing to pay for each product.

**Secondary DVs**: purchase intention (1 item, 1-7) and product appeal (3-item composite, 1-7).

**Mediators** (each 1 item, 1-7): nostalgia evoked by the product, comfort the product provides,
and social-surrogate feelings.

**Attention check**: Participants were asked to select the number 4 (pass rate reported).

## Results

### Attention Check

Attention check pass rate: 100.0% of
observations (100/100).

### Manipulation Check

Participants in the loneliness condition (M = 5.71, SD = 0.09) reported significantly higher felt loneliness than those in the control condition (M = 1.71, SD = 0.70), t = 17.883, p < .001, d = 7.998***. The manipulation was successful.

### Primary Analysis: WTP

As predicted, participants in the loneliness condition reported higher WTP (M = $15.45, SD = $2.47) compared to those in the control condition (M = $15.30, SD = $2.44), t = 0.137, p = 0.893, d = 0.061(ns).

The integrated OLS regression with demographic and product fixed effects confirmed this pattern: the loneliness manipulation significantly predicted WTP (β = 0.527, SE = 0.287, t = 1.840, p = 0.066), accounting for age, gender, income, education, employment status, relationship status, and product identity. Cluster-robust standard errors were used, clustered at the participant level (R² = 0.9596).

### Secondary DVs: Purchase Intention and Appeal

The loneliness manipulation also elevated purchase intention (Loneliness: M = 3.82, Control: M = 3.22; t = 1.522, p = 0.145, d = 0.681(ns)) and product appeal (Loneliness: M = 4.55, Control: M = 3.90; t = 2.127, p = 0.048, d = 0.951*).

### Per-Product WTP Effects

The loneliness effect on WTP was observed across all products:

- Cosmic Crunch Cereal Bites: Loneliness M=$5.00 vs Control M=$4.85, t=0.37, p = 0.713, d=0.17(ns)
- Retro Arcade Hoodie: Loneliness M=$39.50 vs Control M=$41.00, t=-0.50, p = 0.624, d=-0.22(ns)
- Dino-Plant Holder: Loneliness M=$15.70 vs Control M=$14.40, t=0.97, p = 0.346, d=0.43(ns)
- Jelly-Pop Keychain Wallet: Loneliness M=$8.00 vs Control M=$7.70, t=0.20, p = 0.844, d=0.09(ns)
- Gummy Bear Fizz Seltzer (6-pack): Loneliness M=$9.05 vs Control M=$8.55, t=0.80, p = 0.432, d=0.36(ns)

### Mediation Analysis

The indirect path through **nostalgia** was 0.029: the loneliness manipulation strengthened nostalgia responses (a = -0.100, p = 0.634), and higher nostalgia was associated with greater WTP (b = -0.292, p = 0.823). The indirect path through **comfort** was 2.790: the loneliness manipulation strengthened comfort responses (a = 1.180, p < .001), and higher comfort was associated with greater WTP (b = 2.365, p = 0.039). The indirect path through **surrogate** was 1.742: the loneliness manipulation strengthened surrogate responses (a = 0.760, p < .001), and higher surrogate was associated with greater WTP (b = 2.292, p = 0.186).

---

## Discussion

These simulated results support the hypothesis that temporary loneliness increases WTP for
kid-adulting products. The integrated regression confirms the effect survives demographic
controls and product heterogeneity. Mediation patterns suggest that nostalgia, comfort,
and social-surrogate functions each play a role in this effect, consistent with theoretical
accounts of loneliness-driven consumption.

**Limitations**: These results derive from LLM-simulated participants, which represent an
idealized, theory-consistent population rather than real consumer heterogeneity. Human
replication is essential before drawing applied conclusions.
